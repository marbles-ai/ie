About Marbles

Marbles is a small group of passionate engineers and researchers working on the hard Natural Language Processing (NLP) problems plaguing our society. After reviewing existing technologies, we came to realize that there was a lack of robust solutions to the most fundamental of NLP questions: What does this text, written by a human, mean? This may seem like a fairly straight-forward problem, but it's also very difficult to solve. This is clearly demonstrated by today's 'Intelligent Personal Assistants' like Siri and Cortana. These cutting-edge products that run on massive computing clusters can't understand simple queries, and the implementations mainly rely on pre-defined templates.

For example, check out Alexa "Skills". Alexa has a database of known templates that can be expanded with downloadable Skills. When a user speaks to Alexa, their voice goes through a voice-to-text pipeline, and the resulting text is comparted against Alexa's available Skills. There is no understanding of the user's message; the Echo simply does a match to a list of existing strings.

Google's personal assistant does something very similar with "Actions".

Fake News has become a big problem over the past few years. The openness of the Internet has allowed foreign and national actors to hijack information by presenting 'fake news' in an official package. This is particularly sinister, because it is often difficult to differentiate these sources from official sources on the egalitarian Internet.

We have solved these problems by extending cutting-edge NLP research with Machine Learning techniques and representing knowledge extracted from natural language text in a clear representation. We represent information with pieces of knowledge gained from text, and we represent complex ideas and conversations with large networks.

So what does our competition do? Event Registry also uses an NLP pipeline. They use tokenization and Part of Speech (POS) tagging along with entity detection and disambiguation to extract names, dates and events. This allows them to rapidly coellece data from ?hundreds? of sources and look for like terms. This is a useful way of detecting news events and potentially identifying outliers.

Our approach goes much further. We represent the sentences in the raw data in a semantic and discourse-aware representation that alows us to also understand the meaning of the data.
